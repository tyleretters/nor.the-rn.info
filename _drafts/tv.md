Television in the 90's was a portal to another realm.

My earliest memories are of watching *Star Trek: The Next Generation* with my mother, of stealing forbidden glimpses of *Terminator* from my father and uncle, of obsessively watching every Discovery and History channel UFO documentary possible, and of the ever-constant Nickelodeon staples like *Rugrats*, *Rocko's Modern Life*, and *Doug*.

In other words, probably a statistcally normal middle-class American millenial media diet.

I understood the difference between fiction and non-fiction early on, even though shows like *The X-Files* intentionally blurred those lines. I knew cartoons to be creative works designed for kids (except for *The Simpsons*, that wasn't for kids.)  I aso understood TV to be a special privilege, something you only do in moderation or winter. As a kid who  prefered to play in the woods or with legos, TV was often a last resort.

---

Sleep overs were a big deal for us kids. They represented the ultimate manifestation of a play date, the ultimate proclamation of friendship, and the ultimate reward from parental units. There would be nights my friends and I would be up late in the basement and the contemporary programming would eventually run out. Each channel regressed backwards through time. Around 11PM we would arrive at the primordial soup of 1960s Hanna-Barbera classics like The Jetsons and The Flintstones.

But I dig deeper and find something unsettling or perhaps even alien. . I have this lingering sense of... *je ne sais quoi* about the whole experience. 

I knew these cartoons to be different, but couldn't fully appreciate the aspects that made them so.
